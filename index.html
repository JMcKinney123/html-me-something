My Professional Journey

UPS and Bachelor of Arts degree

        While pursuing my undergraduate degree in history and philosophy, I worked as a loader at UPS in St. Louis. The upside to working at UPS was that they paid $1500 a semester toward tuition and books and provided great health insurance at a reasonable cost. Plus, working four hours in the evening was the ideal schedule for most of my undergraduate career. However, once I had to take both day and evening classes to fulfill the requirements of two majors, I had to leave UPS and focused, for a time, exclusively on my education. Upon graduating from McKendree University with a bachelor’s degree in history and philosophy, I had a decision to make: do I want to pursue a graduate degree or begin a career? My goal was to land a government job. However, I was not certain what path I wanted to take. I chose to take some time to consider my options. Ultimately, I decided to return to school and pursue a master’s degree in history, which created a dilemma: do I attempt to find a job that would benefit my resume or focus on my education?

Factory and Graduate School

        I chose to focus on my education and rather than find a job that would benefit my resume, I took a job at a local factory that produced medicated animal feed. A master’s degree in history is quite philosophical in nature. It is essentially a history of ideas. Working in a factory setting allowed me to think about history all day while at work. I wrote some great papers, even presented a couple at conferences, which would have been much more difficult to achieve had I chose to pursue a job that would look good on my resume instead of working in the factory. Looking back on it, I probably should have focused more on a job that would have benefitted my resume. While arguing that Robespierre’s interpellation of ideological subjects inevitably led to his own death was a fascinating symposium paper, being able to clearly demonstrate that I am proficient in Microsoft Office from my employment experience would have been more beneficial in the long-term. Either way, I graduated with an MA in history from SIUE.

MA in History

"

        Before finishing my master’s thesis and, thus, fulfilling the requirements for my MA degree in history, I tried finding a career. My goal was still to get a government job. After applying to over 100 government jobs, it became clear that that was not an option; soldiers returning from the wars in the Middle East had preference over me. Instead, I chose to pursue a career that involved analyzing and interpreting information. Having degrees in history and philosophy, I was well-equipped to analyze and interpret information from multiple points of view. The recession had officially ended by this point, but the unemployment rate remained high. With my diminishing savings account moving ever-lower, I had to find a job. Because I had forklift experience, I decided I would get a job at a warehouse temporarily, while still looking for something utilizing my degrees.

Warehouse

        I remained at that forklift job for nearly four years. It was by no means my ideal job; however, it was the job that allowed me to realize my new goal: buying a home. I worked 60-70 hours a week and made decent money, which allowed me to purchase a lovely home in a great school district for my second grader. As equally important as buying a home, I was now able to move on to my newest goal: finding a new career that involved thought. The days of dumbed down jobs would soon be behind me. I just did not know what career path I wanted to go down. I was talking to my friend on the phone one day, and he told me about LaunchCode. I thought about it for a few months. I was intrigued by the thought of being intellectually challenged once again. It would be completely foreign to me, as my only real experience with a computer is Microsoft Office and 100+ words per minute typing skills. Ultimately, I decided to leave my job and pursue a career as a programmer.
